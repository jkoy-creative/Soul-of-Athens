"Raised to be stoic."

Evan confronts his mental health within the confines of cultural masculinity norms.

Best experienced with headphones.

Its 8 p.m. and Evan Phillips is working on his motorcycle with his best friend Hunter at the storage unit on a Thursday evening in Athens, Ohio. Many college students are typically on their way out to the lively uptown bars, or maybe an underground house show or a date night party as Athens is known for its night time party scenes. Evan’s Thursday night is far from that. Evan is stuck right in the middle of the dark and isolated unit. He is standing by his best friend, Hunter, but knows he cannot open to up him about his struggles, he feels trapped. This is not an unfamiliar feeling for Evan, he has found himself once again not able to open up to someone he considers to be a close friend. He’s aching on the inside, there is so much he wants to say but knows he cannot cross that boundary. He feels comfortable this way because it’s how he’s been conditioned to act his entire life.
Hunter and Evan go home after working on the motorcycles for a few hours. Hunter goes to bed and Evan goes to his room but unfortunately its one of those nights that Evan cannot fall asleep. He lays in his bed staring at the ceiling, moving around from side to side. His mind is racing with uncontrollable thoughts like when an alcoholic walks into a bar at 1 a.m. and starts ordering one drink after the other, he just cannot stop, just like Evan’s mind. He finally grabs his phone and starts to watch Hand Tools Rescue, a YouTube channel about restoring old tools.
“You just hear the tap of a hammer or something similar. It helps that there is no dialogue so then you don’t have to focus on anything. I think old tools are pretty neat. It’s nice to see old rusty things getting restored, it calms me down,” Evan said.
He watches the channel for a few hours and desperately hopes that it will work, but it’s been three hours and Evan’s mind is still racing and he cannot get himself to shut his eyes and let his mind stop thinking. It’s time for Evan to go on a drive.
He gets into his 2002 manual Lexus IS300 and heads towards the Marathon gas station on Stimson Ave. to fill up his gas tank for the drive. He heads towards state route 690 in Athens towards state route 550 until he got to Bartlett. On his way back he took state route 555 to U.S. 50 West to get back to Athens. That was his route for the night.
“This car ride is not for the faint of heart,” Evan said.
It is not an easy drive by any means, there are many narrow and sharp turns. On one of his previous rides he lost complete control of the steering wheel and almost crashed, the roads are that dangerous. But Evan loves it because it gives him something else to focus on, he doesn’t even play music, he wants to stay in the moment of just focusing on the driving, the narrow and dark turns.
The narrower the roads are the more excited Evan becomes. His heart rate goes up, you could almost hear his heart beating through his chest. Every time he changes gears the smirk on his face becomes more visible and you could just tell there is nowhere else in the world that he would rather be. Just him, the road and the steering wheel.
Evan goes for night drives for one or both of the following reasons, 1) If he feels stressed out. 2) If something significant happens in his life and he needs to process it.
“Driving definitely calms me down I mostly do it because it gives me something else to focus on other than whatever I have going on especially when we get on the 555 road it’s very twisty, so it forces you to pay attention so it’s a lot easier for me to just focus on that instead of whatever I have going on in my life,” Evan said. “It’s a good time to think and process stuff too because you don’t have anything else going on at that moment it’s just you and the steering wheel.”
Evan is now on the home stretch he finally makes it to U.S. 50 West, and he is just driving back to Athens, the roads aren’t narrow or sharp anymore, so he uses this time to agonize on his past life.
“I ruminate on stuff that happened in the past just like everybody does, but I tend to focus a little bit more on things that I should probably leave in the past, whether it was relationships or things I did or said,” Evan said. “That is just always something that is on my mind. Whether it’s good or bad, but typically it’s bad.”
Evan finally makes it back home, he tries to be very quiet because his roommates are all asleep. He tip-toes back to his room and he’s finally at a little more ease and is exhausted. He shuts his eyes knowing he has to be up in a few hours for his 8.30 a.m. guitar class.
